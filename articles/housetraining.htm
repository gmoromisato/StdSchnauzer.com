Housetraining

Many years of raising puppies have taught most of the local breeders, that to successfully housetrain your Standard Schnauzer puppy, it is important to:

  1. restrict his or her freedom within the home;
  2. regulate their food and water intake to scheduled times during the day;
  3. take the puppy outside on a leash and assign a command to the action of elimination (e.g. "go potty", "hurry up") when the puppy has started to eliminate;
  4. TREAT and PRAISE every time the puppy goes to the bathroom in the appropriate area; and
  5. that puppy does not have the requisite memory or muscle control to hold his waste products until they are about 16 weeks old - so expect accidents

It is also important to remember the following rules about WHEN to take your puppy outside to eliminate:

  1. when they awaken from a nap;
  2. anytime the puppy has had a hard play session;
  3. within 10 minutes of eating any treat or meal; and
  4. within 5 minutes of drinking water.

In order to train your pup not to eliminate in the house you must be able to keep your eye on him constantly, the easiest way to do this is to restrict him to the room you are occupying. If you can not have the puppy in the room with you where you will be able to watch him, then restrict the puppy to his crate or to the puppy play area you have designed in an area of your home that is quick to clean up in case of accidents, such as the laundry room, kitchen, sun room or any room where it is warm and has linoleum or tile flooring. If you do confine your puppy to a crate, do not do so immediately after a meal or water consumption. A quick rule of thumb on how long a puppy should be able to stay in a crate during the day without soiling in the crate is 1 hour for each month of age the puppy is: a 2 month old puppy (8 weeks) 2 hours; a 4 month old puppy (about the time they get their Rabies shots) 4 hours.

If you are watching your puppy and you start to see signs of him needing to go potty, take the puppy outside immediately. Signs of needing to go potty includes: sniffing, circling or leaving a play area and heading to the edge of the room, GRAB THE PUPPY QUICKLY AND HEAD FOR THE NEAREST DOOR. Male puppies can continue to walk and urinate as they go, so don't hesitate if you see your puppy breaking away from playing to explore.

One of the great advantages of the Standard Schnauzer is that they traditionally house break with ease and once trained, rarely have an accident in the home. Males may mark as they hit their adolescent period (after 8 months of age) or a dog who is ill may have an accident in the home, but these are usually the only times an adult Standard will eliminate inside a home. Additionally, if you adopt an older schnauzer who was raised in a kennel situation, expect some housebreaking issues and accidents the first several months the dog is in your home.

Your pup will be housetrained as quickly, or, slowly, dependent on how diligent you are in monitoring his food and water consumption and how quickly you learn to recognize the puppy's signs of having to go outside.

The other complaint we hear is:

"We were doing so well and suddenly he has begun to wet in the house again"

This happens most of the time because we humans become complacent and think we are such good trainers, that the puppy is "housebroken" before he actually has the maturity to recognize his or her physical sensations of having to go potty and connecting that physical sensation with taking oneself outside to do the job.

Don't be discouraged and don't rush things, keep up the training until you have gone at least four weeks without an accident, at this point you can start to relax the rules a little bit, but one mistake and tack on another four weeks before you loosen the rules again.
