Herding

Introduction to Herding

Why are Standard Schnauzers competing in AKC herding tests and trials?

There was a recent discussion on the Standard Schnauzer web list, prompted by recent successes in herding, at the National Specialty and some regional club workshops that led to this question - schnauzers herding?. The ensuing discussions became centered on exactly why the Standard Schnauzer was or was not a herding breed. If it is a herding breed, why isn't it shown in the AKC Herding Group with Old English Sheepdogs, Collies and Corgis? What is in the Standard Schnauzer breeding or development that generated this herding interest in Standard Schnauzer owners?

And so, I find myself once again writing about a sport that is off the beaten path of those normally associated with our breed. As a Novice in the herding field, I don't pretend to have any expert knowledge about herding, but I am happy to share with you what I have learned from my observations and participation for the past two years.

For the past four years, the Northern California Standard Schnauzer Club has been hosting an autumn herding workshop for its members. I have participated with my Standard Schnauzers, for the last three years and have found I really enjoy this sport. Our local club boasts of having approximately twenty-four members and affiliates who have passed the initial test (HIT - Herding Instinct Test), and many have gone on and passed the secondary test (HCT - Herding Capability Test), and a few have even earned Jr. Herding Titles (JHT) under the auspices of the American Herding Breeds Association.

The Standard Schnauzer is a highly driven dog and according to several other Nations written breed standards. In other countries, the Standard Schnauzer character has been described as "highly developed senses, intelligent, ability to be trained, undaunted-ness, endurance and resistant to weather" (Germany); or as "strong, vigorous, capable of great endurance, alert, reliable and intelligent" (England); and finally, "high-spirited temperament with extreme reliability" (USA). Those descriptive phrases, along with what we know about the breed's development and history as a farm dog, seem to predispose it to herding. Herding was just one more skill the dog developed to help the farmer. The strength and speed and the medium size of the Standard Schnauzer was ideal - it could do the jobs asked of it - ratting, guarding and herding - and till not eat too much or take up too much room in the barn or home or on the carts that went to market.

Those who have participated in a herding test have seen the natural instincts the Standard Schnauzer possess around livestock and how quickly most learn to move the livestock and gather the errant flock member. It is no wonder this breed has tested consistently higher in HIT and HCT evaluations then many traditional herding breeds.

Herding is, in essence, moving livestock in an orderly fashion for a human or under direction of a human. Sheep, goats, cattle, and geese or ducks are animals that dogs are commonly asked to move. Herding should never be confused with chasing. Chasing is a prey-driven activity that benefits the dog in that it is either a fun activity to do or it results in a meal, once the chase ends. Herding dogs should never cause undue harm to the stock they move. There is a moment in the evaluation process where, with guidance from the shepherd, the dog understands that he is working for the human and begins to understand his role is to move the stock, not kill the stock. The "herding light" comes on, as a Judge recently remarked.

There are two distinct styles in herding - one is a field dog and one is that of a stock (or yard) dog. "Field dogs", such as Border Collies or Australian Kelpies, cover vast grazing areas, gathering and moving the stock with the dogs often working in pairs. They move livestock by eye control and stalking techniques. "Stock dogs", on the other hand, work in small corrals, chutes and pens to move stock in close quarters where there is no room for more than one dog. Agility, strength, and speed are essential characteristics for a stock dog; who control the livestock through grab and release techniques, chase, and nipping to elicit cooperation.

In 2004, the AKC has permitted the Standard Schnauzer to begin to participate in its sanctioned Herding Tests and Trials. At the Trial level, the Standard Schnauzer has "for exhibition only" status until such time as there are enough Standards participating at Trials for the AKC to conclude that the breed is in fact able to do the work under Trial conditions competently.

Up until 2004, the Standard Schnauzer participates in herding tests and trials under the auspices of the American Herding Breeds Association (AHBA).

To enter an AHBA trial, the dog must be over six months of age and have passed three levels of testing, under at least two separate Judges, and have received satisfactory scores on the written evaluation for both levels.

Once this is done, you receive a certificate from AHBA to enter trial work.

The initial test is the Herding Instinct Test (HIT). This is the dog's introduction to livestock and under direct control of the Judge in the corral, the dog is evaluated on his interest in the livestock, how long that interest lasts and the dogs ability to move or follow the livestock in some orderly fashion. This test usually involves dogs over six months of age but under the age of 18 months, with little or no exposure to livestock on a regular basis. The purpose of this test is to evaluate the dog's potential to herd and it usually includes some displays in poop eating, lots of barking and lots of wanting to chase. Because the dog is under control of the Judge (usually via a long line), the Judge is able to focus a wandering dog, calm down an overly excited dog and gauge when the dog realizes that it is not there to chase. As mentioned above, the Judge looks for "the herding light comes on" and the desire to control the livestock has replaced he desire to chase the livestock. Some dogs never reach that stage and are not good candidates to continue in this sport.

Level Two of the instinct test is the Herding Capability Test (HCT). This level requires the owner/handler and the dog to work as a team moving the livestock in a controlled manner. The Judge can offer suggestions from outside the arena, but the dog and the handler are alone with the livestock. The dog must respond to verbal commands and directions from the handler, move the stock, gather the stock, and adjust to the movement of the handler.

This test usually requires the handler to move among two pylons (cones / stand alone gates or fences are also used) in a figure eight or half circle pattern, passing by each cone twice. The dog must keep the stock in line with the handler, gather a wayward member of the flock, and not scatter the stock. This level evaluates the dog's understanding of how to move stock. Because there is not a Judge to control the dog, this level requires the dog approach stock calmly, and for the handler to stop the dog and call it to them at the end. When the HIT and HCT are passed satisfactory, the dog can begin further training for trial work.

The evaluation used for the HIT and the HCT covers several areas to gauge the dog's performance.

STYLE: How the dog moves the livestock. Gathering style means the dog keeps the stock together and headed in line with the handler Driving style keeps the stock together but the dog works between the handler and the stock.

Inexperienced dogs can show both gathering and driving styles in one test, but will usually settle in to one style as they get more exposure to stock.

APPROACH: Manner the dog uses to near the stock. Wide is the ideal approach for it alerts the stock to the dog's presence without scattering or frightening the stock Close is the usual Standard Schnauzer approach and it causes some scattering and may cause the dog to work harder than needed during the test or a trial.

EYES: Eye contact with the stock and concentration on its movements Strong eye is that demonstrated by Border Collies and is identified by its stalking, fixated gaze with no acknowledgment of the outside world.

Loose eye is a dog that has good concentration without the intensity or inability to see or hear the outside world.

WEARING: The back and forth moving needed to keep the stock grouped and stop the splitting off of members.

BARK: A silent dog is the preferred worker. Barking is interpreted by many breeds of sheep as inexperience on the dog's behalf. These sheep breeds will turn and challenge the barking dog or refuse to move.

TEMPERAMENT: Refers to the dog's ability to do the job even with outside distractions.

INTEREST: Concentration on the livestock and its movements

POWER: Ability to control the stock and work with them, even if they don't want to work for the dog.

RESPONSIVENESS: Listens to handler and accepts training and directions

GROUPING OF THE STOCK: Judges the consistency of the dog to keep the stock moving in a group and regroup straggles or fleeing stock.

BALANCING STOCK TO HANDLER: The dog's position in comparison to the handler and the stock.

STOCK EVALUATION: Behavior of the stock and willingness to work for the dog that is in the arena.

Some final thoughts for novice herding dogs and handlers;

Number One ---- practice, practice, practice.

Most Standard Schnauzers herd sheep exclusively, although some have demonstrated the tenacity to work cattle; so, learning to gauge where you are, as a handler, in relationship to the stock and your dog is something that is not only necessary to succeed in trial work, but it will help prevent injury to yourself, your dog and the livestock.

Although you may think you are lucky if you find a training facility that has sheep that stick with the handler regardless of the dog's antics - these are nicknamed "Velcro sheep" - you should plan on training with sheep that are of a lighter temperament and more flighty, as you gain confidence. Sheep with lighter temperaments are the ones you will most likely encounter at tests and in the trial arena and so it is vital that you gain verbal control over your dog in order to gain the trust and control over the stock. Which leads us to final thought number two?

Number Two --- Get Verbal Control over Your Dog

We have many CD and CDX obedience titled Standard Schnauzers in the Northern California Club who have gone deaf in the herding ring and since your main job as a handler is the protection of the sheep, you have to control the dog.

Some basic commands needed for herding are:

  • STAND (Stay or Wait) - needed to stop the dog from charging the sheep on the initial approach;
  • OUT (or Leave It) - tells the dog not to get so close to the stock;
  • BACK -- step back further or go back and gather an errand flock member;
  • AROUND (Away to Me and Come-By) - move to the left or right of my position;
  • WALK UP - approach slowly from the back of the herd; and
  • THAT'LL DO - enough, we are done, lets go home.

If nothing else start with very strong COME and STAY commands during training. The other herding commands are easy to work into training sessions as the dog will perform them naturally as training progresses.

Verbal control is the preferred control method for starting out in herding, but some dogs will not heed to a voice command in the heat of the herding passion and you will need to resort to "blocking" the dog with a shepherd's crook. If blocking isn't working you may need to shift to the "rattle paddle" which is an oar-like plastic paddle with ball bearings inside. You can re-direct the dog by banging the paddle on the ground to gain the dog's attention or by physically moving the dog away with the paddle end placed against the dog's side. Some dogs will require a physical correction upon the body to obey the handler. Because most Standard Schnauzers are fast learners, it is rare that you will have to resort to a physical correction to get the dog's focus.

Standard Schnauzers perform herding with the same enthusiasm and style that they do everything else in their lives. Some styles I have witnessed over the past two years are amazing. I have seen ten and twelve month old Standard Schnauzers enter a ring and never chase the sheep as prey. They just seem to have an innate sense of what they are in the arena to do. Others are more interested in sniffing the ground and the world around them and never engage the sheep at all.

Others will approach the sheep with mouths open ready to bite - these dogs usually are recommended for cattle because of their hearty dispositions. Dogs have been kicked at and had to duck to avoid getting hit by the hooves of a high-flying run away sheep. Others become so frenetic that at the sight of the livestock that no one can get them to understand they are in the ring to "herd", they only want to chase. Ironically, some of the hardest dogs in the home are the calmest in the area, and visa-versa.

Prepare to have your toes stepped on and nearly tripped by passing sheep, your dog will go deaf to your commands on occasion and your dog will back talk and challenge you every step of the way. This is a sport that utilizes the dog's natural instincts, power, drive and agility and for those of us in the suburbs and the cities, it is a nice escape for a few hours - being on a farm and working with livestock in a manner befitting the Standard Schnauzer's rural past.
